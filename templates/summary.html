By the numbers

Schools at a Glance

There a variety of schools serving various purposes in the US and its territories. At a high-level, it's important to know what is going on with these schools. What's their distributions look like for different data? Would someone know whether schools are generally expensive or cheap, and would they be able to afford it? Is it easy to be admitted into a school based on the type of person you are? Questions like these require an initial understanding of the data, and hopefully we can provide some of that insight here.

There are some interesting variables to comb over in the college data, which lead to interesting conclusions. To get started, there are some fun facts about the dataset

  • Did you know that there over 7300 accredited institutions in the US and its territories? There are over 35 major programs offered across the institutions.
  • Over 50% of students get Pell grants when applying for colleges - makes the financial aid easier to get!
  • Student populations on average tend to be around 1.5% veterans, but some have special incentive programs to allow for more attendance.

School Types - An Overview

There are three primary school types in the dataset - Public, Private, and For Profit. It's important to make a distinction between the different school types for the high-level data visualizations since each is different in their education models.

Public schools are open to all students and funded through local, state, and federal taxes. They tend to have a variety of student population sizes and provide relatively cheap ways to get a full education.

Private schools are those that are funded primarily through tuition and grants, but don't operate as profit-institutions. They have high tuition but also provide extensive research and internship opportunities.

For Profit are schools that tend to serve specific purposes, such as additional professional training and vocational education. Most tend to be smaller schools that focus on one area and allow for professional specialization.

School Types - The Finances

For most prospective students and their families, costs in attending schools are an incredibly important consideration to make. Across the US, there are a variety of family situations and schools which are available to attend for those families so the different types of schools can provide varying cost benefits.

It is important to note that to put things into perspective, several statistics are needed. To begin with, the average net cost for all schools is $16,200 per semester, including tuition, books, campus fees, and more. For a two year education, this ends up being almosts $65,000, which can be a heavy load for families. As a result, almost 50% of students apply and qualify for Pell grants, which is surprisingly high for a prosperous and developed country. But this becomes obvious when the average family income of $38,490 for students attending all school types is taken into consideration.

School Admissions - Capability and Identity

One of the most nerve-wracking aspects of the application process is figuring out schools which are within the range of attendance. Test scores, grades, resumes, and activites all combine to create a "holistic" view of the student, removing the dependency on the student being "just a number". However, it is inevitable the students get analyzed based on their test scores and other metrics. Within the US, the SAT - originally the Scholastic Aptitude Test - is the premier test to determine students positioning. As such, it is interesting to see that, out of a max possible 1600 (for this dataset), the average score across students is 1132. An alternative test, called the ACT, is similarly used for assessment. It is scored out of 36, and across the US, students taking the ACT average a 23.

With the US, there are a variety of races represented, since the US prides itself on being a "melting pot" of cultures and people. While the country is primarily Caucasian, there are still large representations from different races, specifically Black and Hispanic citizens. This country-level grouping can be seen in the variety of college students who enter different schools across the country. As it is to be reasonably expected, there are around 50% white students across all schools, though this is different from the around 75% white total population. A discrepancy like this can be explained by the fact that some schools are located in U.S. territories, and tend to have 100% native populations, which are not white.
